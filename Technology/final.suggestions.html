Summary of Activities,
Recommendations, Suggestions, and
Outstanding Questions



President's Task Force on
Strategic Technology and Communications Planning



Report Prepared by David Feldman
1 December 1999


This list summarizes the activities of the president's task force on strategic technology and communications planning. This list is not prioritized; i.e., item 1 is not necessarily more important than item 2.

1.
Assessment of Short-Term Communications Technology Needs of the Faculty: Early in the Spring term of 1999, we conducted a brief discussion and survey of faculty's technology needs. Specifically, we asked what technology faculty needed to help them teach better. This discussion was brief, and focused on short term needs. It was not, by any means, a comprehensive review of technology needs. The following items received more than one vote. The numbers in parentheses indicated the number of votes an item received.

(a)
Outfit every classroom with working A.V. equipment (11)
(b)
Body I can Call - Person to call for troubleshooting, etc. (8)
(c)
Organize and/or expand slide collection. (4)
(d)
Money to expand film collection (4)
(e)
Teaching space with computers (4)
(f)
Computers in offices that can access reasonably current versions of programs. (4)
(g)
Long-term equipment plan. (4)
(h)
Reliable Internet access from home (4)
(i)
satellite dish with international programs (4)

2.
Responses to perceived needs of the faculty:

  • Items (a), (c), (d) and (e) have been summarized and submitted in a separate memo for consideration for title III or other granting opportunities.

  • Prior to the creation of the technology task force, it had been decided to hire an additional staff person for computer services. This addresses item (b).

  • Item (f) has been addressed by the most recent wave of computer upgrades.

  • Our response to item (g) is discussed in item 3, below.

  • The question of modems and dial-up access was a frequent topic of discussion during the year-long existence of the task force. The faculty as a whole has repeatedly said that dial-up access from home is a high priority. The task force has not been able to come to consensus on how to proceed.

  • Item (i) has been addressed by the international studies committee.

3.
Equipment Acquisition Proposal: In our brief discussion in the faculty meeting and elsewhere, we frequently heard a desire for a long-term equipment plan. In response to this, we have drafted a proposal for a procedure through which faculty can purchase equipment. This proposal has approved unanimously by our task force and has recently been submitted to Jonathan Ehrlich and the Academic Affairs Committee.

4.
Recommendation that academic affairs facilitate a discussion of the future directions of academic and educational computing at COA: We were never able to undertake an assessment of longer-term educational technology needs; in this sense one of the main goals of our task force has not been achieved. In our discussion with faculty, we gained the sense that there were some basic needs that weren't being met. There was some division within our group about whether or not it was appropriate to proceed with a long-term assessment of technology goals without also simultaneously addressing some of these shorter term concerns. At this point, however, we believe that many of these short-term needs have been addressed. We strongly encourage the Academic Affairs Committee to facilitate a broad discussion of how technology should and should not be used to further the educational mission of the college.

5.
Recognition of the ongoing need to facilitate, chart, budget for, and manage of technology across the campus: In addition to the above item concerning educational and academic technology, there is an important, ongoing need to establish a clear process for the articulation and communication of technology priorities. It is the general sense of the task force that some sort of regular group needs to be established to chart and help manage and plan for the ongoing, cross campus uses of technology by students, staff, and faculty. However, the working group was not unified in its sense of how best to do this.

6.
Recommendation that a group of faculty, staff, and students be convened to explore the possibility of a computer classroom on campus: It is our strong sense that a computer classroom would greatly extend and improve the ways we use technology to help us teach and learn. By computer classroom, we have in mind a multi-use teaching space designed specifically to incorporate computers. A wide range of faculty, staff, and students have expressed interest in such a facility. A computer classroom will have a broad curricular impact and will be used by a large percentage of students. Perhaps most importantly, a computer classroom will let us explore technology in ways that we haven't been able to do previously. Thus, we feel that a computer classroom is a logical next step for the college. We recommend the formation of a group of interested faculty, staff, and students to further clarify what we want and then work with the director of development, the academic affairs committee, and the academic dean to figure out how meet our goals.

7.
Recommendation that the physical condition of classrooms be monitored: During our many discussions of technology, a strongly recurring theme among faculty was the need for adequately equipped classrooms. In addition to ``high tech'' items like overhead projectors and vcrs, there seemed to be significant concern about such things as blackboards, chalk, and lighting. We encourage the academic affairs committee to be vigilant in making sure that our teaching spaces are appropriately maintained and supported.
