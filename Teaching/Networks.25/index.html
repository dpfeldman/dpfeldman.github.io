Network Analysis and Modeling

David P. Feldman

College of the Atlantic, Spring 2025

Basic Info

Network science is an active and growing cross-disciplinary area that focuses on the description, representation, analysis, and modeling of complex social, biological, and technological systems as networks or graphs. At its simplest, a network is a collection of nodes, some of which are connected by edges.

For example, nodes might be scientists, and there would be an edge connecting two scientists if they co-authored a scientific paper. Or nodes might be facebook users, and there would be an edge between two users if they were facebook friends. Structures and systems modeled as networks are ubiquitous in the world around us: communication networks, networks of friends and acquaintances (online and in-person), gene regulatory networks, supply chains, and food webs, to name just a few.

In general, networks are used as models in situations in which the architecture of connectivity matters, but where that connectivity is neither random (as in an ideal gas or non-interacting agents) nor regular (as in a crystal lattice or a situation where agents interact spatially). Capturing, modeling, and understanding networks requires understanding both the mathematics of networks and the computational tools for identifying and explaining the patterns they contain.

This course will consist of a survey of techniques for modeling and analyzing the structure and dynamics of networks. We will begin with basic definitions and simple descriptive statistics. We will then look at random graphs, which are useful for generating intuition and serving as simple null models. We will then consider network prediction models that can be used to predict node attributes and missing edges, and approaches for detecting community structure. As time permits, we will examine dynamical models on networks, such as disease and rumor spreading. Throughout, we will take a computational approach, learning how to work with network data and how to implement algorithms for network models and data analysis.

Evaluation will be based on participation in class sessions, coding exercises, and a final project.

This class meets the QR requirement. It does not meet the ES requirement.

Goals and Expectations

  1. Stay physically and mentally healthy and maintain intellectual and personal connection during a potentially difficult time.
  2. Gain a grounded introduction to various techniques in natural language processing, including word frequency analysis, semantic embeddings, sentiment analysis, and topic modeling.
    1. Understand qualitatively how these methods work.
    2. Be able to implement these methods using existing python packages.
    3. Understand the strengths and limitations of these methods.
  3. Gain experience posing research questions that can be addressed with computational text analysis, particularly questions that explore how racism, sexism, nationalism, and colonialism manifest in bodies of texts. This includes situating and contextualizing your research and presenting your results.
  4. Improve your programming skills and analytical/computational thinking.
  5. Have fun while growing and learning.

Participation and Engagement

We will do a lot of different things in our class sessions, including discussing readings. The readings we do will range widely in content, style, and difficulty. There are several goals and motivations we have for discussions:

  • The discussions are a chance to figure out some things together. We will be doing some readings that are sometimes technically and/or theoretically difficult and subtle and, we hope, interesting. Working together, we can solidify and deepen our understanding.
  • In some of the discussions it will be particularly valuable to hear a diversity of opinions. We expect there will be a range of reactions and responses to some of what we read, and there is tremendous value to actively listening to and engaging with a diversity of thought and experiences.

What your Evaluation is Based on

Your evaluation will be based loosely on the following:

  • Class participation and engagement: 20 percent
  • Short homework exercises, coding and otherwise: 20 percent
  • Leading paper discussion: 10 percent
  • Final project: 50 percent
In general, we recommend against grades; we believe they are more likely than not to interfere with genuine, reflective learning. This is especially true in this class, where we have found that many of the class activities really don't lend themselves to letter grading. We do not have any quota of A's, B's, etc.

More Course Info and Advice

Structure and Pacing

  • It is perhaps best to think of this course as a workshop/seminar.
    • It is a workshop in that we'll be doing lots of experimenting, trying things out, and so on. Expect to be doing lots of coding, de-bugging, re-thinking, and so on, both in groups and individually.
    • It's a seminar in the sense that there will often be readings and/or exercises for class, and we'll spend class discussing and comparing ideas and results.
  • Relatedly, we will not be following a textbook, nor will be there be many homework assignments to hand in.
  • The syllabus is fluid and the pacing and exact topics we cover will depend on the students in the class.

What this Class Isn't

  1. This is not a class on artificial intellegence (whatever that means).
  2. We will not cover large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, etc.
  3. This is not an introductory programing class, in the sense that it's not designed to systematically cover a standard set of introductory coding topics and skills. That said, the course does cover some coding techniques, and so could be a good chance to "level up" your coding skills -- including for those who have no coding experience.

Help/Coding Sessions

Once we get into the more programming-intensive part of the course, there will be help/coding sessions. You are warmly invited and encouraged to attend these sessions. Help sessions are relaxed, informal, and hopefully fun. Things that happen at help sessions:

  1. The TAs and/or I am around to offer help on assignments and the final project.
  2. Some students work in groups at help sessions, others work more or less alone.
  3. Help sessions are also a chance to ask general questions about the course. Conversations also sometimes wander into other areas: politics, the state of the world, what's for dinner, what classes are offered next term, and so on.
  4. Help sessions are a great way to meet other students in the class.
  5. Almost always there will be coffee/tea and/or snacks.

Individual Meetings

We are happy to meet with students one-on-one. The best way to set up an appointment is to send an email. There are lots of reasons why you might want to meet:

  1. You have some in-depth questions that there isn't time to explore in a help session.
  2. You have a question that you think is too basic or you're uncomfortable asking in a help session. (You shouldn't be uncomfortable, since, as the saying goes, there are no dumb questions! But we understand that you might be uncomfortable nevertheless.)
  3. You want to explore possibilities for your project.
  4. You want to discuss some challenges you're facing in the class.
Please don't hesitate to reach out if there's anything you want to discuss. You should also feel free to reach out to the TA.
