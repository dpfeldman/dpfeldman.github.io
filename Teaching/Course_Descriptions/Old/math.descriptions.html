Description of Courses

These descriptions are unofficial; they are not intended to replace the course descriptions found in the course catalog.


Math for Human Ecologists

Instructor: Don Cass.

This is not a remedial course. It is designed for students who have a reasonably solid background in algebra and who wish to gain more experience using math to describe the real world. Topics covered will include: making and interpreting graphs, logarithms and exponential growth, and elementary statistics. Students who have difficulty applying math to concrete problems may find this course helpful before taking biology, physics, chemistry, or economics courses. Math for Human Ecologists is not a prerequisite for these courses, however.

Math for Human Ecologists can also serve as a "pre-calculus" course for those wishing to hone their algebra skills before entering the calculus sequence. Students should be aware, however, that serving as a gateway to calculus is not the primary function of Math for Human Ecologists.

Don is using a different textbook for the course this year. The new text is "Functioning in the Real World." A copy is available in the library. I believe it's presently located in the new books section. The new text does not signal a radical reworking of the course; however, the course will be slightly different than versions of Math for Human Ecologists offered in the past.


College Algebra I

Instructor: Bo Greene.

From a handout Bo will give to students on the first day of class:

This class is for students who have had algebra and probably algebra 2 in high school. It is not a remedial math course, but is intended to bring students to a reasonable level of comfort and competency in concepts of algebra, either for use in other classes or for its own sake. This class may serve as a refresher for a subsequent precalculus course, but will not, on its own, prepare students for calculus.

It is assumed that you have taken high school math, but many of you may not be satisfied with your level of mastery. It has also probably been a long time since you took (or used) math and you may suffer from the "use it or lose it" phenomenon.


Calculus I

Instructor: Dave Feldman.

This goal of this sequence of courses is to develop the essential ideas of single-variable calculus: the limit, the derivative, and the integral. Understanding concepts and applications will be emphasized over "plug and chug" problems and intricate mathematical maneuverings. The mathematics learned will be applied to topics from the physical, natural, and social sciences. There is a weekly lab/discussion section. We will use either computers or graphing calculators; no experience with these is necessary, however.

Students entering this course should have a solid background in algebra and functions. Students should have taken precalculus in high school, or math for human ecologists, or have had an equivalent course. Those who have questions about their preparedness should see me (Dave).

At least half of the grade in this course will be based on weekly homework assignments. There will be exams, but they will probably be open-book and untimed.


Calculus II

Instructor: Dave Feldman.

This course is the continuation of Calculus I. It begins by considering further applications of the integral. We then move to approximations and series; we conclude the course with a brief treatment of differential equations. The mathematics learned will be applied to topics from the physical, natural, and social sciences. As in Calc I, at least half of the grade in this course will be based on weekly homework assignments. There will be exams, but they will probably be open-book and untimed. It is likely that I will give students the option of completing a final project instead of a final exam.

Students who have had some calculus already and are considering taking Calc II without Calc I should see me (Dave.) We will be covering topics in a slightly untraditional sequence. I.e., Calc I at COA includes some topics that often aren't seen until Calc II in traditional courses, and vice-versa.
